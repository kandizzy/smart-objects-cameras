# Outsider Interview: English Communication Coach

Student: Yuxuan Chen  
Project: English Communication Coach / Ambient English Feedback Object  
Interviewee: My roommate, an international student  
Format: In person  
When it happened: May 1, 2026  
Note: This interview was conducted in Chinese. The quotes below are translated as closely as possible from what she said.

## What I showed

I showed my roommate the working prototype of English Communication Coach. I did not explain the whole project at first. I let her click around and tell me what she thought it was doing.

## Interview

### 1. Before I explain anything, what do you think this tool is doing?

At first, she asked:

> “What is this?”

After clicking around for a little bit, she said:

> “It looks like something related to English learning.”

This showed me that the interface communicated the English support direction, even before I explained the full classroom context.

### 2. Who do you think this tool is for?

She said:

> “Maybe it is for people whose first language is not English.”

Because she is also an international student, she connected the tool to second-language speakers very quickly.

### 3. Does this feel helpful, stressful, or a little bit of both?

She said:

> “I don’t feel stressed. I think it is helpful.”

Then she explained:

> “As a person who speaks English as a second language, in daily life I often have some grammar problems, or some expressions are not very natural.”

She also said:

> “But my classmates are all very polite, so they will not point out my problems.”

And:

> “Sometimes I may never know that some sentence I said was wrong, so my English will not improve.”

She said she wants faster improvement:

> “In daily life, no one will point out my problems, but I want my English to improve quickly. I also want to learn more natural English expressions.”

This was important because she did not see private feedback as pressure. She saw it as something missing from daily life.

### 4. If you were speaking in class, would you prefer feedback right away or after the session?

She said she wanted both.

> “When I am speaking in class, I want immediate feedback, and I also want feedback after class.”

She explained why immediate feedback matters:

> “Immediate feedback is useful because after class I am usually too busy. I probably will not go back and check what I said wrong in class.”

She also said:

> “If it can give me gentle feedback at that moment, I can immediately catch what I said.”

But she also wanted after-class feedback:

> “At the same time, I also need after-class feedback, because sometimes in class I cannot be distracted. I still need to focus on what the teacher is saying.”

This helped me understand that the timing should be flexible. The system should not interrupt, but the student may still want a gentle option during the speaking moment.

### 5. Does this feel private and supportive, or does it feel like monitoring?

She said:

> “I don’t think this feels like a monitor, because this is private.”

She also said:

> “It does not expose my problems to other people.”

And:

> “I would not feel like everyone knows I am always making mistakes.”

She explained why it felt private:

> “It only appears on my own computer, so for me it is private. It also feels supportive.”

This response supported my design direction. The feedback should stay personal and should not be shown publicly.

### 6. What kind of feedback would be most useful to you: grammar, clarity, wordiness, vocabulary, or presentation flow?

She said:

> “For me, grammar and vocabulary are the most useful.”

She explained:

> “I always only use words I already know. My vocabulary is limited.”

She also said:

> “I always use familiar words to explain things, but I want to use more natural vocabulary.”

This helped me see that vocabulary upgrade is not just an extra feature. For international students, vocabulary can directly affect how clearly and naturally they express ideas.

### 7. For international students, do you think this kind of private feedback would make them more willing to speak in class?

She said:

> “Yes, I think so.”

Then she explained:

> “If international students have this kind of thing to support them, they really may be more willing to speak.”

She said some students are not quiet because they do not want to talk:

> “Some people are not introverted. It is not that they do not like talking. They are just afraid they will say something wrong.”

She also said:

> “They may worry that other people will not understand them, or that they did not express clearly, or that they said many things wrong.”

She described this as a cycle:

> “The more they do not dare to speak, the worse their English becomes. And the worse their English feels, the more they do not dare to speak. It becomes a bad cycle.”

This was one of the strongest comments from the interview. It connected my project to confidence, not only grammar.

## What surprised me

I expected her to prefer only after-class feedback, because I was worried that in-the-moment feedback might feel stressful. But she wanted both immediate and after-class feedback.

This surprised me because it showed that “real-time” feedback is not always bad. The problem is not only when the feedback appears. The problem is whether the feedback is gentle, private, and optional.

## What I learned

This interview helped me understand that private language feedback can feel supportive if the student controls it.

I also learned that international students may not get enough honest language feedback in daily life. Classmates are often polite, so they do not point out grammar or vocabulary problems. Because of that, students may repeat the same patterns for a long time without knowing.

For the next version of English Communication Coach, I want to keep:
- private feedback
- gentle tone
- optional timing
- grammar support
- vocabulary suggestions
- after-class review

This interview confirmed that the project should not feel like public correction. It should feel like a private support object that helps international students speak more and improve over time.
