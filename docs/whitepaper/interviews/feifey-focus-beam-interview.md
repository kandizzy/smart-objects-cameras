## Why this person?

I chose Jason because he’s not in interaction design or this class, but he’s familiar with classrooms and presentations through his film work. He hasn’t seen this project before, so his reactions are more instinctive rather than informed by prior context.


## What you showed them

I gave him a very short one-line setup, which I told him that this is something for helping people follow slides in class and then showed a live demo of the system running on my laptop. I used my hand to point at different parts of the slide while the system responded in real time, without explaining how the sensing worked beforehand.



## The conversation

### 1. Before I tell you anything — what do you think this is doing?

> It looks like you’re controlling which part of the slide is visible, like everything else gets dimmed and that part stays clear. I can’t tell if you’re doing it manually or if it’s following your hand though.

### 2. What do you think it's *for*? Who would use it?

> Probably for teaching. Like lectures or presentations. I’m imagining a big classroom where people in the back can’t see clearly, so this helps guide attention.

### 3. What was confusing or unclear?

> At first I didn’t know if it was reacting to your hand or the slide itself. Like I thought maybe it was pre-programmed. Also sometimes the highlighted area felt a little off from where you were pointing, so I wasn’t totally sure how precise it’s supposed to be.

### 4. What surprised you — good or bad?

> The dimming is nicer than I expected. It doesn’t feel harsh, it just kind of naturally pulls your eyes in. But when your hand moves quickly, it feels a bit jumpy, like it’s trying to catch up

### 5. If you walked into a room with this running, would you even notice it? Would you want to?

> I think I’d notice it after a bit. It’s not super obvious right away, but once you see it, it makes things easier to follow. I wouldn’t find it distracting, which is good.

### 6. What would make this feel creepy or invasive? What would make it feel useful?

> If I thought it was watching people, like tracking faces or attention, that would be weird. If it’s just tracking the teacher’s hand, that’s fine. It feels useful when it lines up with what the person is actually talking about.

### 7. What's missing? What did you expect it to do that it doesn't?

> I kind of expected it to move a bit smoother, like less snapping between areas. And maybe handle when you’re talking about multiple things at once? Right now it feels very one point at a time.

### 8. <Your own question — something specific to Focus Beam that you genuinely want an outsider's read on>

> I think it’s like a softer version of a laser pointer. A laser is very exact and intentional. This feels more happening on the background, like it’s guiding you instead of telling you exactly where to look. I don’t think it replaces it completely, but it could for most situations.

---

## What I learned that I couldn't have learned from a classmate

Jason instantly understood the core idea without explanation, but he framed it as a “softer laser pointer,” which made me realize I’ve been thinking of it more as an automated system rather than a communication tool. 
He also pointed out the emotional quality of the dimming. At the same time, his confusion about whether the system was pre-programmed or reactive showed that the input for focus beam isn’t as legible as I assumed, and the small inaccuracies are much more noticeable to outsiders than to me.



## One quote worth keeping

> “I can’t tell if you’re doing it or if it’s following you, but either way it makes it easier to look at.”

— *<Jason>*

## What you'd change based on this

I would focus on improving the smoothness and responsiveness of the highlight movement to reduce the feeling of jumpiness. I would also explore ways to make the system’s automation more legible, so users can more clearly understand that it is reacting to gestures rather than being manually controlled.
