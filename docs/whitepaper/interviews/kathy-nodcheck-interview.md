# Outsider Interview

**Student / interviewer:** Kathy
**Project:** NodCheck
**Camera used:** Laptop webcam
**Interviewee:** Shannon, my roommate
**Their relationship to the class:** None
**Date of interview:** May 2, 2026, ~8pm
**Format:** In person
**Length:** ~20 minutes

---

## Why this person?

Shannon is my roommate and has zero connection to the Smart Objects class. She had no idea what I've been building and came in completely cold.

---

## What you showed them

I originally tried to have Shannon use her own laptop as the student device, but kept running into issues getting both laptops on the same network. Eventually I just sat her in front of my laptop and asked her yes or no questions while NodCheck tracked her movements. The only thing I told her was: "I'm going to ask you some yes or no questions, just nod yes or shake no." I wanted to see if the system would pick her up before explaining anything.

---

## The conversation

*We started with a live demo. There were a few minutes of troubleshooting, camera angle, Shannon sitting too far from the screen, and the failed two-laptop attempt, before detection started working consistently.*

**Kathy:** I'm going to ask you some yes or no questions, just nod yes or shake no.

**Shannon:** OK.

**Kathy:** Do you have a pet?

*(Detection not triggering due to camera angle. Kathy repositions the laptop.)*

**Kathy:** Do you have a pet?

**Shannon:** *(nods yes)*

**Kathy:** Did you eat breakfast today?

**Shannon:** *(nods yes)*

**Kathy:** So basically, the movements of your head are being tracked to confirm a positive or negative response to the questions being asked.

**Shannon:** Exactly, yeah.

**Kathy:** So this idea came from a real experience. We were in class and my teacher was trying to teach something and it was just completely silent. Eventually she asked "does everyone understand?" and I was the only one who shook my head no. And she said "thank you, I knew there were some blank stares." So I created this to help teachers figure out whether to slow down or keep going. I'd love your take on it.

**Shannon:** Would the students be getting the feedback? Like, sent to their device?

**Kathy:** No, just the instructor. I figured it should be low stakes for the students to be honest. I wanted to create this kind of invisible approach, not have them feel pressured to speak up if they're not comfortable.

**Shannon:** Yeah, I think that's a good way to keep the whole class from being equally lost.

**Shannon:** My bigger concern is how do you engage students so that they feel comfortable to actually do it? Like who's to say that students will even nod their head. In your class, no one spoke up to begin with, but you were still the only one that even nodded. Would it be something that's stated to the students that it's being used, so that they would know that all they have to do is nod yes or no?

**Kathy:** I haven't fully flushed this out yet, but for our class we do a lot of tech demos on our laptops, so I figured it would run during those times. It's more of an extension than an application. I know it's a little bit surveillance, but at least during those moments people kind of know that's what's happening.

**Shannon:** Yeah. I guess the question is whether students know they have the option to just nod, and that that's all they need to do to communicate with the teacher in that situation.

**Kathy:** In my prototype there's a faint prompt on the screen that says "nod yes or no" but it's so faded you probably didn't notice it. Maybe it's one of those things where the class does it a few times and just gets used to it, especially during tech demos where they'd come to expect it.

**Shannon:** Yeah, totally. Especially if it's during tech demos, they'd just expect it.

**Shannon:** I actually thought of Zoom when you described it, because that's an environment where it's more intimidating. It requires a hand raise, you don't know if you're being seen on screen, it's hard to read the environment. So I feel like in that case it would be super helpful.

**Shannon:** It feels a little bit like it's detaching the human to human interaction though. Like my bigger concern is how you engage students so they feel comfortable doing it.

**Kathy:** I guess I'm also just curious if you see any other blind spots with the concept.

**Shannon:** I'm thinking about going further, and I know this might not be feasible, but even reading microexpressions. Because sometimes people won't admit to being confused, which is kind of why this even started. A lot of people don't mask as well as they think they do. That feels really ambitious, but it would give you a really honest read.

**Kathy:** That's a really good point. Originally I thought the cameras on the classroom ceilings could detect this, but they wouldn't catch facial expressions. It would have to be on the laptop, which is what everyone has in front of them during class. And I don't like having my laptop open during lecture, so it would really depend on the context.

**Shannon:** Yeah totally. But that's a really good point to think about.

**Kathy:** This was really helpful, thank you. And I think everyone in my class has just been so sucked into their own projects, talking about the same things over and over. An outside perspective helps.

**Shannon:** Yeah, that's fun!

## What I learned that I couldn't have learned from a classmate

Shannon made me realize that just because the tool lowers the stakes doesn't mean students will actually use it. She brought up that in the moment that inspired this whole project, I was still the only one who responded, even with just a head shake. Hearing that from someone outside the class made me actually stop and think about it. Everyone in Smart Objects has been so deep in their own work that we've stopped questioning the basics. Shannon also brought up Zoom completely unprompted, which hadn't been on my radar as a use case at all. That was a new angle.

## What you'd change based on this

Shannon's feedback made me realize the onboarding moment matters just as much as the detection itself. If students don't know the tool is running or don't know that nodding is all they have to do, it doesn't work. But more than that, Shannon made me think about the difference between lowering the stakes and actually removing the barrier. NodCheck assumes students will use it, but the barrier to participation might not just be about speaking out loud. It might be about any kind of response at all. So the question I want to keep thinking about is how do you create an environment where students feel comfortable enough to engage in the first place, even in the lowest stakes way possible.
