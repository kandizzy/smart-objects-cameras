# Vignette: Bruno uses the timer

**Student:** Phil

**Project:** Timer

**Camera used:** Horizon

**When it happened:** Mon Apr 27, ~6:30pm

**Who was in the room:** Bruno, Carrie, rest of class

---

## The moment

> Bruno is at the whiteboard. Carrie is sitting in the seat at the back of the room. The rest of the class is seated around the tables in their usual spots. We're working on our smart classroom storyboard. The timer runs out and we decide to give ourselves another 15 minutes to ideate. In `whiteboard mode`, the room feels alive with ideas. The students and teacher participate and contribute freely. 
>
>Phil set the first timer, now it's Bruno's turn to try. Phil lets Bruno figure out the interaction for himself using the icons taped to the arUco tags. Bruno sets the timer by aiming the central tag's arrow at the 15-minute mark. He flips the `play` tag and the timer display on Phil's laptop confirms it's running. Carrie and Phil exclaim, "Yes!" — excited by the fact that the smart classroom is beginning to come to life.

## Why this moment mattered

>Just seeing a screenshot of `timer_web.py`, one might miss the perspective of the shared experience of **using the timer as a group** — which allows the group a moment to reset and align before starting (or continuing an ideation session.)
>
>To set a timer — or more generally — to set a limitation to an ideation session, is to free oneself from wondering, **"are we spending too much time on this?"** 
>
>Because the countdown display was only visible to me, Bruno had to rely on only the markers on the board; staying engaged with the board vs. stepping off to the side. I like that it made setting the timer a group activity — and a novel moment in the ideation session — to see the instructore test out a new interaction.

## One supporting artifact

*No artifact — I was so excited to see the timer being used by someone else, I didn't stop to document.*
