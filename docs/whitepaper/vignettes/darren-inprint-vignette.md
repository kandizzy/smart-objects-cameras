# Two moments in the Room : Inprint

Story 1
Like a few weeks ago, the program had a workshop in our classroom and covered all of the tables with a paper covering. At first I honestly didn't think much of it. It was boring plain paper over table. But as time went on, the table began to change. At first it started with little drawings. During class I would draw little doodles on the table during lectures (paying attention of course). At this point in the semester, everyone had their own unofficial assigned seats - a few favorite spots around the class room tables. And what was interesting about this new table is that you can start to see little personalization's and doodles left by that's section's most seated student (most seated is that a phrase?). Obviously since, we don't have official assigned seats, people often moved around. And this is where the magic happened. Doodles started interacting with other doodles. This wasn't all. People started writing notes on the table as well. During group work, I noticed one group writing out diagrams directly on the table. And the best part is that people were sometimes doing it unconsciously. Yes, we had a whiteboard. But that takes set up, clean up. Mostly importantly, it was erased after every session.

Those tables to this day are a living classroom artifact; notes encompassing a diverse set of insights and personal touches contributing to a growing creative ecosystem.

## Why these moment mattered

I really like writing things out, I feel like it's something comes more naturally than doing everything digital. The digital shift has squished us to the limitations of our laptops and our phones. Yes everything can be transferred cross-platform. But there is always a tool you need to learn, always multiple programs you need to have open. This comes with a lot of other issues like portability issues due to different types of files, have to work within software constraints, etc.

Drawing with a writing tool is an instinct that comes more natural to us. At least, that's what I believe. You have more creative freedom through just plain sketching. You only need a pen/paper, and it encourages deeper collaboration. The issue, for a while, has been portability. With Inprint, I want to bridge that gap between freeform writing and drawing and make that portable on both digital and analog mediums.
